The Thoughtful Pruning of a Coquitlam Willow: Why Patience Makes Perfect

Tree Pruning Coquitlam, BC Silverback Treeworks

Willows are among the fastest-growing trees in BC's residential landscape. What makes them beautiful — their vigorous, sweeping growth — is also what makes them challenging to manage near structures. This is the story of a willow pruning job in Coquitlam, and why we planned it over two seasons rather than cutting it all back at once.

Understanding Willow Growth Habits

Willows are the sprinters of the tree world. They grow rapidly and send up enthusiastic new shoots — what arborists call epicormic growth. A single aggressive cut that removes too much foliage at once stresses the tree and triggers an even more vigorous regrowth response. The result is usually worse than the original problem.

Our approach on this Coquitlam job had three objectives:

  • Create 2 metres of clearance from the building
  • Reduce excessive shoots without harming the tree's natural form
  • Plan for a two-stage process — some work now, more next season

The Art of Selective Pruning

The "hack it back" approach some crews use on willows is counterproductive. Instead, we:

  1. Identified key branches that could be trained away from the house with directional cuts
  2. Made precise cuts just outside the branch collars to promote proper wound healing
  3. Thinned rather than topped — removing select shoots to allow light and air penetration through the canopy
  4. Left enough foliage so the tree could continue feeding itself through photosynthesis
"With willows, you need to work with their growth habits, not against them. That's why we're spacing this work over two seasons." — Silverback Treeworks lead arborist

Responsible Cleanup

Every clipping was carefully processed. Small branches were chipped for mulch; larger pieces were offered to the homeowner for firewood; remaining debris was hauled away. We don't leave sites with debris piles — cleanup is part of the job.

5 Signs Your Coquitlam Tree Needs Professional Pruning

  • Touching structures: branches within 2 metres of roofs, windows, or siding
  • Uneven or excessive growth: one side significantly denser, or water sprouts crowding the canopy
  • Dead or diseased limbs: brittle, leafless branches or discoloured bark
  • Safety concerns: low-hanging branches over walkways or obstructed sightlines
  • Poor airflow: dense foliage blocking sunlight to lower branches, or moisture buildup encouraging moss

For willows, maples, and other fast-growing species, annual light pruning consistently produces better long-term results than drastic cuts every few years.
